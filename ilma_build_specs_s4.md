## BUILD 2: HIPP Analysis Template — AP World History
**Priority:** HIGH
**Students served:** Emma Cotner, Saeed Tarawneh, Ella Dietz, Jackson Price (entire AP World cohort)
**Subject:** AP World History

### The problem
Every at-risk AP World student struggles with document-based questions (DBQ). The DBQ requires HIPP analysis (Historical context, Intended audience, Purpose, Point of view) for each source document — but the current course teaches HIPP as a concept without providing structured practice applying it to actual documents. Emma's Skill 2 (Sourcing) is at 0%. Jackson and Ella both need DBQ technique, not more content.

### What to build
A structured QTI exercise that walks students through HIPP analysis step by step for each document, with scaffolded fields:

**Format for each exercise:**
1. Student reads a primary source document (text excerpt, image, or political cartoon)
2. Student fills in 4 fields:
   - **H — Historical context:** "What was happening at the time this was created?" (1-2 sentences)
   - **I — Intended audience:** "Who was this created for?" (1 sentence)
   - **P — Purpose:** "Why did the author create this?" (1 sentence)
   - **P — Point of view:** "How does the author's position shape this document?" (1 sentence)
3. After submission, student sees a model answer for comparison

### Requirements
- 15-20 exercises using actual AP-style source documents spanning Periods 1-9
- Documents should include a mix of: text excerpts, political cartoons, maps, data tables
- Each exercise is standalone (not part of a full DBQ)
- Include model answers so students can self-assess
- This does NOT need to be graded for accuracy — it's a skill-building drill, not an assessment

### Done looks like
A student opens the module, reads a document, fills in the 4 HIPP fields, submits, and sees a model HIPP analysis for that document. They do this for 15-20 different documents across world history periods.

## BUILD 3: Source Interpretation Practice — AP US History
**Priority:** HIGH
**Students served:** Cruce Saunders IV (primary), other APUSH S4 students
**Subject:** AP US History

### The problem
Cruce scored 4 on the APUSH practice test. He is one SAQ technique fix away from a 5 — his essays are already the strongest of any at-risk student (DBQ 5/7, LEQ 5/6). Two sets of QTI exercises:

**Set A: Political Cartoon Interpretation (10 exercises)**

**Set B: Primary Source Excerpt Interpretation (10 exercises)**
- Student reads a 1-2 paragraph excerpt from a historian, political figure, or reformer
- Student answers: "The author is arguing that ______ because ______" (structured response)

- Model answers should demonstrate the difference between "what the source says" (wrong) and "what the source argues" (right)
- This does NOT need to be timed or graded — it's a skill drill

### Done looks like
A student opens the module, views a political cartoon or reads an excerpt, writes a structured interpretation, submits, and sees the model answer. They do 20 total (10 cartoons + 10 excerpts).

## BUILD 7: "Identify-Explain-Connect" Rhetorical Analysis Drill — AP English Language
**Priority:** HIGH (but see dependency)
**Students served:** Sara Beth Way (primary)
**Subject:** AP English Language and Composition
**Dependency:** Subject DRI coaching should start FIRST — this drill reinforces what coaching introduces

### The problem
Sara Beth scored 2 on the AP Lang practice test. She can label rhetorical devices (Skills 1+2 STRONG at 60-100%) but cannot trace how they work or what effect they create (Skills 5+6 CRITICAL at 10% and 25%). On her FRQs, she writes things like "the author uses metaphor" but never explains HOW the metaphor works or what EFFECT it creates. This is the core skill the AP Lang exam tests.

Her +18% retake inflation (56% first-attempt → 74% all-attempts) confirms the current course structure rewarded recognizing terms on retry, not performing analysis.

### What to build
A structured QTI drill where each exercise forces the three-step analytical move:

**Format for each exercise:**
1. Student reads a short AP-style prose passage (1-2 paragraphs)
2. **IDENTIFY:** "What rhetorical device or strategy does the author use in the highlighted section?" (select or short answer)
3. **EXPLAIN:** "HOW does this device work in this specific passage? What does it do to the reader's understanding?" (2-3 sentence response, not a label)
4. **CONNECT:** "How does this serve the author's overall purpose or argument?" (1-2 sentence response)
5. After submission, student sees a model response showing the full identify-explain-connect chain

### Requirements
- 10-15 exercises using short AP-style passages
- Passages should cover a range of rhetorical situations: persuasive essays, speeches, literary criticism, satire
- The "identify" step is the easy part (Sara Beth can already do this) — the model answers should emphasize the "explain" and "connect" steps
- Include model answers that show the difference between surface-level labeling ("the author uses ethos") and analytical depth ("the author establishes credibility by citing her 20 years of field research, which makes the reader trust the counterintuitive claim that follows")
- Each exercise should take 5-8 minutes

### Done looks like
Sara Beth opens the module, reads a passage, fills in the three fields (identify, explain, connect), submits, and sees a model response. She can compare her "explain" and "connect" answers to the model to see if she went beyond labeling.
